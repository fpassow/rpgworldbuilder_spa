RPG World Builder

Try it!

Do you have ideas for role-playing games, but wonder if you can turn them into something you can really play with your friends?

Are you an experienced GM, who has run a few different rules systems, and maybe one or two you've created yourself?

If you answered yes to both questions above, this site is for you. The tool on the next page will help you flesh out you idea, and help ensure that it has what it takes to drive weeks of enjoyable campaigning.

I call this a world builder and a campaign creator because it's about setting and situation, not rules. You already have books full of rules. You can get free and universal systems online. And you can probably write some yourself if you have to.

The hard part is to come up with ideas that are:

  1. Creative and exciting
  2. Practical and playable

Below, I list the fields on the form and explain what they are and how they work.

You can create an account to save your work. And if you create an account, you can also clone and modify other peoples' ideas, or add them into your own.

  • Clone: Copy all the text from any campaign into a new campaign owned by your own account.
  • Add-To: Copy all the text from any campaign and add it, field by field, to a campaign that already exists in your own account.

On to the fields...

Title

Always include a working title. It's evocative. And it's the name that appears on a saved campaign. And you can change it any time you want.

Seed Text

Images, concepts, fragments, design goals, movie references, songs... anything. Record everything that is resonating with you and might become part of the campaign.

Changes

It's easier to have exciting events in the PCs' lives when the world itself is changing. For a shorter campaign, a change can define the setting. War, exploration, the rise of magic, the vanishing of magic, or the Zombie Apocylipse. Don't be afraid to drop the PCs into Interesting Times(TM).

Mood

Grim, exciting, zany, lyrical, tactical, dream-like, happy, sad, desperate, exuberant, etc. How will the players feel?

Your game will produce some feeling. So make it intentional.

Visual Style

How do you see it in your mind? Does it look like a favorite movie, comic, artist, etc.?

You can use this as inspiration for many details later on.

Scope

A city, a planet, a war, a subculture, etc.

Try larger or smaller physical scopes than you are used to.

And scope isn't just an area on a map. A single great city could be the setting for campaigns about court intrigue, ambition among wizards, and about shape changers living in the shadows and sewers. But the limits and rules and social landscape of each campaign could be different.

End

Does this scenario have an end? An impending end can add focus and intensity. And you can always create another campaign for the same PCs, unless you actually destroy the entire world. Or you can destroy the entire world. Isn't this fun?

Drivers

These are patterns which help you write playable adventures.

Fiction writers may sneer a "formula" fiction. But they have total control over their characters. When playing with your actual, live, creative, and surprising friends, you need some structure to let you plan some things in advance.

A clear formula also helps the players and speeds up play. We've all had that slow starting game where the players can't quite find the adventure. So a little clarity makes everyone's live better.

Of course, you can run any kind of adventure in your actual campaign. Drivers are an engine and a safety net, not a cage.

You can also use these as inspiration, or as a design challenge. Pick two drivers, along with the culture, technology, and magic found in the world... then try to make it work.

Agents

PCs work for someone who sends them on adventures. James Bond works for MI6. Rebels work for the Rebel Alliance. Brave knights work for their king. And Indiana Jones works for the archaeology department at Marshall College.

PCs might be part of a rigid chain of command. Or they might be mostly independent and self motivated. The key is that adventures are part of their job.

The PCs can be an elite team, or new recruits. Or they might work in an obscure corner of some vast bureaucracy. And the varieties of jobs are endless. Consider The X-Files, Men in Black, Stargate, Sanctuary, Outland, Judge Dee, Agents of Shield, Battlestar Galactica, all the Star Treks, western sheriffs, Blade Runner, Mission Impossible, and Torchwood.

Freelancers

Paid freelancers include Ghostbusters(tm), private detectives, bodyguards, bounty hunters, assassins, hackers, mercenaries, etc. PCs can be guns for hire, swords for hire, spells for hire, cyberdecks for hire, or have fast ships, or other vehicles, for hire.

The PCs are contacted by their customers, and paid to go on adventures.

Privateers

Privateers were like pirates who operated during a war. They had permission from their side to attack and rob ships belonging to the other side. And the field of legalized crime is fertile ground for adventuring.

In many fantasy RPGs, PCs seek out members of an "enemy" species, kill them, and take their stuff.

Robin Hood was technially a bandit. But occupiers and oppressors can excuse plenty of fun and profitable crime.

Quest

A journey to achieve a goal. Quests may seek a magical treasure, a cure, or a personal fortune. The Lord of the Rings trilogy is a quest to destroy the ring. Battlestar Galactica is a quest for the legendary lost colony and a new home.

Survival

PCs may be lost or marooned away from their own kind, trying to stay alive and find a way home. Castaways can be transported by fantastic means and can find themselves in very strange places. Examples: John Carter of Mars Lost in Space, and Farscape. A large group of castaways might turn into a colony. And exiles are deliberate castaways.

Or PCs might man a lonely outpost in a dangerous land, or simply try to stay a live in a deadly, futuristic urban wasteland. PCs might be hated, hunted, or tracked by a relentless foe. Survival might also depend on meeting a special need, such as a vampire's need for blood.

Survivors explore their environment, fight off attackers, and seek vital resources. They meet new friends and allies, and new enemies.

Personal Mission

Or "personal quest". The PCs are doing something because they just really want to do it, or because they deeply believe it should be done.

They are driven to become the world's greatest swordsman, to advance science, prove the Hollow Earth is real, or bring back Atlantis.

A PC with a personal quest and a personal fortune can employ like-minded PCs as Agents.

The PCs' personal mission could also be an extended hunt or mystery.

Escape

A kind of Quest. A desperate journey to Anywhere Else. Powerful, but best for short campaigns.

Merchants

Business isn't always boring. Think of the early spice traders in Earth’s Age of Exploration, the caravans along the Silk Road, or maybe the fur traders of the American frontier.

Keys Quest

A classic… The Great Magic Thing was divided into parts. The parts were scattered and hidden in dangerous places. And the PCs have a reason to find them.

Like all quests, it has a final end. But it has a bunch of intermediate ends, too. Twelve parts automatically gives you twelve adventures. Stretch it to a few adventures per part and you can get a whole year of play.

It’s over-used. But it’s over-used because it works.

Wandering Heroes

PCs have explicitly signed up to fight evil and help those in need. Examples: Knights of the Round Table, Dr. Who, The A-Team.

This is a popular classic. But make sure the players explicitly agree to it, because it requires the PCs to do dangerous things without any other rational motivation.

Treasure Hunters

Treasure hunting is popular in fiction and a straightforward source of adventures.

However, treasure hunters who are after the Big Score either get tired of failing, or succeed and retire. So don't count on treasure hunting alone for a long campaign.

Toys and Treasures

Imaginary toys should be awesome.

Imagine a player saying, "My character has an XYZ", or, "We use the XYZ". If you imagined them smiling when they said it, your campaign needs the XYZ.

Of course, awesome doesn't have to mean powerful and perfect. Ask anyone to name three D&D wands, and one of them is sure to be Wand of Wonder.

Common categories include...

Weapons

Players like weapons. Big weapons. Powerful weapons. Strange and surprising weapons. Weapons that have unusual effects, or which make silly noises.

Ammunition

Finding your third +1 sword isn't exciting. But everyone wants another box of grenades. Ammunition, potions, rare spell components, dilithium crystals, and other useful consumables are a GM's friend.

Defenses

It's easier for players to move beyond just killing everything if they don't actually have to.

Healing

Medic!!!

PCs care about anyone or anything that can heal their combat damage. And healing potions and med-paks are a consumable resource, too.

Tools

Toys that don't kill people. Or at least can do other things, besides killing people.

Since weapons have a head start on coolness, try to pump up the awesomeness of your world's tools. And give yourself and extra pat on the back when the PCs solve a problem with a tool instead of their weapons.

Spells

Spells names and effects should reinforce the overall style of the game world. They can be baroque, practical, horrific, playful, flashy, subtle, etc. Magic is whatever you want it to be.

Magic Items

See Spells for notes on style.

And magic items excel at quirkiness and unpredictability. They can do whatever you want... it's magic. And you decide who gets to use them.

Vehicles

Vehicles are a great enabler, especially flying ones.

Animals

Riding animals, witch's and wizard's familiars, etc. They are useful. And they are NPCs which you can play.

Magic Powers

Faeries, werewolves, and superheros all have special powers that make them fun to play.

Natural abilities

Wings, gills, night vision, etc. Also consider extreme yet non-magial abilities, such as those of the Mentats and Bene Gesserit of Frank Herbert's Dune.

Cyber Powers

We can rebuild you... with extra awesomeness.

Skills

Humans can learn to do many things. And being exceptionally good at something is pretty cool.

Training

Players care about increasing skills. Use that interest. You can have mentors, apprenticeships, schools, guilds, and the secret technique of any group.

And magic and cyberpunk offer their own special options.

Social status

People like you, or obey you, or fear you, or at least have a taboo against killing you, etc.

Usually, status comes with membership in some group.

Authority

Like social status, but with teeth. Of course, there may be situations in which the authority doesn't apply...

Example PCs

Describe a few PCs you might enjoy playing. These don't have to be wonderful or iconic or anything. They point is just to see how easy (or hard) it is to create fun PCs in this world.

Adventures

How can you run some of these classic adventure types?

Assault

Attack a place to achieve a goal. Often there's a planning an preparation phase, followed by sneaking and/or combat, sometimes followed by running away.

Siege

Defend a place, and maybe some NPCs, a valuable location, etc. Common phases are discovering the threat, finding and reaching the refuge, preparing defenses, and multiple waves of attacks with recovery and repair in between. PCs might be rescued by sunrise or the cavalry. Or they might weaken their foes enough to make a run for it, or even just win outright.

Recon

Go somewhere dangerous. Gather information to help plan future action. Get out alive.

Rescue

Get a person or people (or creatures) safely away from people (or creatures) who are holding them prisoner. The people you rescue might be weak or strong, helpful or difficult.

Theft

Get a thing from the other side, without necessarily having to fight them.

Escape

PCs have been captured. Most of their equipment has been taken away. And now they try to get away. Escapes can be a nice change of pace where the PCs concentrate on getting out of a place instead of getting in. Other adventures can turn into escapes if the PCs are captured. And a successful escape can double as a recon or an all out assault from within.

Gauntlet

The PCs have to get from point A to point B, possibly while transporting something or someone. And lots of nasties will try to stop them. A gauntlet adventure usually has a linear form, and a clear goal. The pace is fast because the PCs will lose if the enemy has time to organize and concentrate its forces. The theme is overwhelming odds... just not faced all at once. :-)

Hunt

The PCs track/stalk/chase something that (initially) hides and runs away. The hunt can be literal or metaphorical. And the tables might be turned. But the PCs are generally the pursuers. The PCs can themselves be hunted. But this is harder to run and works best when combined with other adventure types.

Monster Mystery

Bad things are happening. Investigate. Find the monster. Deal with it.

Mystery

As in mystery fiction. Sherlock Holmes, etc. PCs are detectives who investigate and solve the mystery.

Explore

Visit all the parts of a dangerous place to find out what's there. Classic dungeon crawling is Treasure Hunters and Privateers Exploring an underground setting.

Capture

A Hunt in which someone or something must be taken alive. This is often more complicated than just killing them.

Trick

And elaborate deception or "con". The PCs must design, prepare, and execute all the parts of a plan. Each step is played out under that constant threat of being discovered. And the PCs might have to improvise if something unexpected happens.

Negotiate

The PCs must identify, locate, contact, and talk to multiple parties to achieve their goal. Combat isn't the point. But threats are a common tactic. And negotiations might break down.

Compete

You can add a competitor to other kinds of adventures. Or build the adventure around competition for some otherwise straightforward goal.

Character Classes

Types of people in this world, who they are, and what they do. The rules don't have to be class based. Classes are just an awesome way to tell players about a world.

PC Creation Notes

Tell the players who they are, and who they can be.

First Adventures

Describe a possible first adventure for this campaign, plus a few more that you would enjoy running.

Parts you love

What are your favorite parts from what you' written so far? These are things to focus on going forward. Or, if you don't know what to write here, then set this idea aside for a while to give your imagination time to generate some more Good Stuff.

Parts the players will love

Think of the people you usually play with. What will each of them love about this campaign? Be sure to include those things in the final version. Or, if you don't know what to write here, think of each person and what they might enjoy.
